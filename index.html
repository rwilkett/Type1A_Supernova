How is a Type Ia different from other supernovae?

Supernova "types" were named for their spectra long before the physics was understood: Type I = no hydrogen lines, Type II = hydrogen present. Type Ia additionally shows a strong silicon absorption line. Mechanically, though, the real divide is different: a Type Ia is a thermonuclear bomb, while Types II, Ib and Ic are all core-collapse events — Ib/Ic are simply massive stars that lost their outer layers before dying.

Why are they a "standard candle"?

If you know how bright a light really is, how faint it looks tells you its distance (the inverse-square law). Astronomy's hardest problem is finding objects whose true brightness is knowable — standard candles.

Type Ia supernovae qualify for one beautiful reason: every one detonates from nearly the same starting point. The Chandrasekhar limit is a universal constant, so each explosion burns a near-identical fuel load into a near-identical mass of ⁵⁶Ni — and ⁵⁶Ni sets the peak brightness. Core-collapse supernovae, whose progenitors span a huge range of masses, have no such common starting point.

They are not perfectly uniform — but the leftover scatter is predictable: brighter events decline more slowly. Measuring the decline rate (Δm₁₅, the Phillips relation) calibrates each event to ~6% distance precision.

Add their extreme luminosity — briefly outshining ~5 billion Suns, visible across most of the observable universe — and they extend the cosmic distance ladder far beyond any other rung. Comparing their distances against redshifts revealed in 1998 that the universe's expansion is accelerating — the discovery of dark energy (2011 Nobel Prize in Physics).
